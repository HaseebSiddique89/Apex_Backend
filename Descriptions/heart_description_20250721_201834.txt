2.  The heart is a muscular organ responsible for pumping blood throughout the body via the circulatory system. It is located in the thoracic cavity, between the lungs, and is crucial for delivering oxygen and nutrients to tissues and removing carbon dioxide and waste products.

3.  The heart comprises four chambers: the right atrium, right ventricle, left atrium, and left ventricle. The atria are the receiving chambers, while the ventricles are the pumping chambers. Blood enters the right atrium from the superior and inferior vena cava, then passes to the right ventricle. The right ventricle pumps blood to the pulmonary artery. Oxygenated blood returns to the left atrium via the pulmonary veins and flows into the left ventricle. The left ventricle, the strongest chamber, pumps blood into the aorta, which distributes it throughout the body. The heart also contains valves (tricuspid, pulmonary, mitral, and aortic) that ensure unidirectional blood flow. Major vessels connected to the heart include the aorta, pulmonary artery, pulmonary veins, and vena cava.

4.  The heart's primary function is to pump blood throughout the body, ensuring oxygen and nutrient delivery to tissues and the removal of carbon dioxide and waste products. The right side of the heart pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs for oxygenation, while the left side pumps oxygenated blood to the rest of the body. The heart also regulates blood pressure and maintains proper circulation.

5.  The heart beats approximately 60 to 100 times per minute in adults at rest, circulating about 5 liters of blood per minute. Cardiovascular diseases, such as coronary artery disease, heart failure, and arrhythmias, can impair the heart's function. Maintaining a healthy lifestyle through regular exercise, a balanced diet, and avoiding smoking can help prevent heart-related issues. An electrocardiogram (ECG) is a common diagnostic tool used to monitor the heart's electrical activity and detect abnormalities.
