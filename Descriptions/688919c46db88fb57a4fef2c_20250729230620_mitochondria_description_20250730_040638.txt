1.  The object in the image is a mitochondrion.

2.  A mitochondrion (plural: mitochondria) is a membrane-bound cell organelle found in the cytoplasm of eukaryotic cells. It is often referred to as the "powerhouse of the cell" because it generates most of the cell's supply of adenosine triphosphate (ATP), used as a source of chemical energy.

3.  The structure of a mitochondrion consists of several key components:
    *   **Outer Membrane:** This is the outermost boundary of the mitochondrion and is relatively smooth. It contains porins, which are channel-forming proteins that allow the passage of molecules smaller than a certain size.
    *   **Inner Membrane:** This membrane is highly folded into structures called cristae, significantly increasing its surface area. The inner membrane is less permeable than the outer membrane and contains proteins involved in electron transport and ATP synthesis.
    *   **Cristae:** These are the infoldings of the inner membrane. They increase the surface area available for the electron transport chain and ATP synthase, thereby increasing the mitochondrion's ability to produce ATP.
    *   **Intermembrane Space:** This is the space between the outer and inner membranes. It plays a crucial role in establishing the electrochemical gradient used for ATP production.
    *   **Matrix:** This is the space enclosed by the inner membrane. It contains mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA), ribosomes, enzymes, and other molecules involved in the Krebs cycle (also known as the citric acid cycle) and oxidative phosphorylation.

4.  The primary functions of mitochondria are:
    *   **ATP Production:** The main function is to generate ATP through cellular respiration, which includes the Krebs cycle and oxidative phosphorylation.
        *   The Krebs cycle occurs in the matrix and produces electron carriers (NADH and FADH2).
        *   Oxidative phosphorylation takes place on the inner membrane, where the electron carriers donate electrons to the electron transport chain, creating a proton gradient across the inner membrane that drives ATP synthesis.
    *   **Regulation of Cellular Metabolism:** Mitochondria participate in various metabolic pathways, including the breakdown of fatty acids (beta-oxidation) and amino acids.
    *   **Calcium Homeostasis:** Mitochondria play a role in regulating calcium ion concentration within the cell, which is important for signaling processes.
    *   **Apoptosis (Programmed Cell Death):** Mitochondria can initiate apoptosis by releasing certain proteins into the cytoplasm.
    *   **Heat Production (Thermogenesis):** In some cells, mitochondria can produce heat instead of ATP through a process called non-shivering thermogenesis. This is particularly important in brown adipose tissue (brown fat).

5.  Additional information:
    *   Mitochondria have their own DNA (mtDNA), which is circular and similar to bacterial DNA. This supports the endosymbiotic theory, which proposes that mitochondria were once free-living bacteria that were engulfed by early eukaryotic cells.
    *   Mitochondria can replicate independently of the cell cycle through a process called binary fission.
    *   Mitochondrial diseases are genetic disorders that result from mutations in mtDNA or nuclear DNA that affect mitochondrial function. These diseases can affect various organs and tissues, especially those with high energy demands, such as the brain, heart, and muscles.
    *   The number of mitochondria per cell varies depending on the cell type and its energy requirements. Cells with high energy demands, such as muscle cells, can have thousands of mitochondria.
